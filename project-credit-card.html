Credit Card Default Prediction

View repository
Concepts

Project Overview

Using the UCI Default of Credit Card Clients dataset, I built models that predict whether a customer will default next month. Instead of relying on demographic attributes, I focused on behavioural patterns that indicate financial stress.

Approach

1. Behavioral features instead of demographics

Credit risk should be guided by how customers manage their accounts. I excluded demographic variables and used only financial behaviour such as bills, payments, credit limits, and repayment history.

2. Engineered features that measure financial stress

Monthly bill and payment columns are noisy and highly correlated, so I built a small set of summary features that better reflect repayment behaviour:

  • Times Delayed: Number of months in the last six where the account was late. This captures how often the customer falls behind.
  • Average and Maximum Delay: Average lateness across six months and the single worst delay. Together they show both typical behaviour and the most severe incident.
  • Pay Trend: Difference between the most recent delay code (PAY_1) and an older one (PAY_6). Positive values mean repayments are getting worse over time, negative values mean they are improving.
  • Utilization and Payment Ratios: Current bill relative to credit limit, payment relative to bill, and average bill and payment amounts. These features summarise how heavily the card is used and how much of the balance is usually repaid.

These engineered signals make it easier for models to capture patterns that raw columns may hide.

3. Models tuned for imbalanced data

Since most customers do not default, I oversampled defaulters in the training set. I evaluated logistic regression, Random Forest, SVM, and KNN using accuracy, precision, sensitivity, F1 score, and AUC on a held-out test set.

Key Results

Random Forest provided the strongest balance of performance, improving sensitivity on defaulters from the mid thirties to above fifty percent while keeping AUC near 0.77.

This matters because identifying high-risk accounts early is more valuable than maximising overall accuracy. The results also show that good predictive performance is possible using behavioural data alone, which aligns better with responsible lending practices.
